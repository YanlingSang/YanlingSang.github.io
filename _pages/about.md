<p style="margin: 6px 0; line-height: 1.6; font-size: 16px;">
👋 I am a Master's student in the Civil Engineering Department at the <strong>University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA)</strong>, specializing in <strong>Transportation Engineering</strong>. I earned my Bachelor's degree in Traffic and Transportation from <strong>Beijing Jiaotong University</strong> in collaboration with <strong>Delft University of Technology</strong> in 2024.
</p>

<p style="margin: 6px 0; line-height: 1.6; font-size: 16px;">
My research lies at the nexus of <strong>large language models (LLMs)</strong>, <strong>urban mobility</strong>, and <strong>intelligent transportation systems</strong>. Spanning from foundational methodologies to the emerging frontier of connected and autonomous vehicles, my work centers on four interconnected themes:
</p>
<ul style="margin: 6px 0 6px 20px; line-height: 1.6; font-size: 16px;">
  <li>🤖 <strong>LLMs for urban science:</strong> Architecting intelligent agents for urban discovery, adaptive planning, and transportation decision-making</li>
  <li>🧠 <strong>AI-driven behavioral modeling:</strong> Developing models that learn, replicate, and synthesize complex human travel patterns and activity chains</li>
  <li>🚦 <strong>Mathematical optimization for transportation networks:</strong> Formulating and solving network design, vehicle routing, and resource allocation problems through operations research, mathematical programming, and metaheuristic algorithms</li>
  <li>🚗 <strong>Intelligent transportation systems:</strong> Engineering algorithms for real-time prediction, planning, control, and cooperative driving in connected and autonomous multi-agent environments</li>
</ul>

<p style="margin: 6px 0; line-height: 1.6; font-size: 16px;">
Ultimately, I am driven by a vision to pioneer <strong>data-driven, statistical, and AI methodologies</strong> that transform transportation infrastructure into <strong>connected, autonomous, intelligent, and human-centered</strong> <strong>mobility ecosystems</strong>. These systems not only optimize <strong>mobility</strong> efficiency, accessibility, and equity but also reveal and reshape the underlying social dynamics that govern urban transportation and city life.
</p>

    <p style="margin: 6px 0; color: #64748b; font-size: 15px;"><strong>Research Assistant | Advisor: Asst. Prof. Manxi Wu, UC Berkeley | May 2025 - Oct 2025</strong></p>
    <p style="margin: 6px 0; line-height: 1.6; font-size: 16px;">
      • Leveraged NYC's January 2025 <strong>Central Business District Tolling Program (CBDTP)</strong> and Lyft's temporary $1.50 rider credit as a natural experiment to examine platform strategies and traveler responses<br>
      • Built an OD–hour–day panel with Uber, Lyft, and MTA subway data; applied <strong>two-way fixed effects regressions</strong> with parallel-trend validation to identify causal impacts<br>
      • Found Lyft trips rose 2–5% during the subsidy while Uber trips fell 2–6%; total ride-hailing declined 0.5–1.5% and subway ridership increased about 1%, indicating partial modal shift<br>
      • Uncovered distributional effects: short and low-fare trips fell by &gt;35%, while platforms (especially Uber) saw 8–19% revenue gains; passengers paid more and drivers saw limited gains<br>
      • <strong>Outcome:</strong> Manuscript submitted to <em>Transportation Research Part A</em> titled "Congestion Pricing in New York City: Effects on Ride-Hailing, Transit, and Welfare"
    </p>
